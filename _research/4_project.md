- Abstract: We develop a spherical freezing model as an exact analogue of a radiation-dominated Friedmann universe, extending the planar freezing--lake analogy to fully three-dimensional geometry. By considering the quasi-static growth of an ice sphere into a supercooled liquid and applying the Stefan condition, we derive a growth law $$\dot{R} \propto 1/R$$, leading to $$R(t) \propto t^{1/2}$$. This yields a direct correspondence between the radius $$R(t)$$ and the cosmological scale factor $$a(t)$$ reproducing the Friedmann equation $$H^2 \propto a^{-4}$$ and an effective equation of state $$w = 1/3$$. We then generalize the model by incorporating buoyancy-driven convection, which breaks spherical symmetry and induces axisymmetric deformations of the interface. Expanding the interface in Legendre modes and introducing a convective enhancement via a Nusselt factor, we derive coupled evolution equations for the mean radius and anisotropy. The resulting dynamics map onto an effective Friedmann equation with a modified equation of state $$w_{\mathrm{eff}} = 1/3 - 2\beta$$, where $$\beta$$ characterizes convective scaling. Anisotropic contributions generate a shear term scaling as $$S^{-6}$$ for $$\beta = 1/3$$, in direct analogy with Bianchi-I cosmology. This framework provides a minimal and physically transparent laboratory analogue for both isotropic and anisotropic cosmological expansion, highlighting how transport processes and geometry jointly determine effective cosmic dynamics.
